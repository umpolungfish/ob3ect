## 1. Background: Gaucher Disease and Glucocerebrosidase

Gaucher disease, the most common lysosomal storage disorder, results from deficient activity of glucocerebrosidase (GBA, EC 3.2.1.45), the enzyme responsible for hydrolyzing glucosylceramide (GlcCer) to glucose and ceramide. The disease manifests in three clinical types:

| Type | Onset | CNS Involvement | Prevalence |
|------|-------|-----------------|------------|
| Type 1 (non-neuronopathic) | Adult | No | 1:40,000–60,000 |
| Type 2 (acute neuronopathic) | Infant | Yes | 1:100,000 |
| Type 3 (chronic neuronopathic) | Child | Yes | 1:100,000 |

Current therapy consists of enzyme replacement therapy (ERT) with recombinant GBA (imiglucerase, velaglucerase alfa, taliglucerase alfa) administered intravenously every two weeks. Limitations include high cost (~$300,000/year), infusion reactions, and inability to cross the blood-brain barrier for Types 2 and 3.

The Rebis-optimized GBA variant presented here offers a path toward mRNA-encoded enzyme therapy, enabling sustained endogenous production of a catalytically optimized enzyme.

## 2. Catalytic Mechanism: Glu-Glu Retaining Glycosidase

Glucocerebrosidase belongs to glycoside hydrolase family 30 (GH30), employing a **retaining double-displacement mechanism**. Unlike the Ser-His-Asp triad of serine proteases, retaining glycosidases use **two glutamic acid residues** as the complete catalytic machinery:

### 2.1 The Two Glutamates

| Residue | Role | Position in WT GBA | p$K_a$ shift |
|---------|------|-------------------|--------------|
| Glu340 | Nucleophile | C-terminal end of $\beta$-strand 7 | Elevated from 4.2 to ~6.5 by hydrophobic environment |
| Glu235 | Acid/Base | C-terminal end of $\beta$-strand 4 | Maintained at ~4.5 by electrostatic environment |

### 2.2 Reaction Sequence

**Step 1 — Glycosylation (formation of covalent intermediate):**
1. Glu340 (deprotonated, nucleophile) attacks the anomeric carbon (C1) of the glucose moiety
2. Glu235 (protonated, acid) donates a proton to the glycosidic oxygen
3. A covalent $\alpha$-glucosyl-enzyme intermediate is formed
4. The aglycone (ceramide) leaves

**Step 2 — Deglycosylation (hydrolysis of intermediate):**
1. A water molecule enters the active site
2. Glu235 (now deprotonated, base) abstracts a proton from water
3. The hydroxide attacks the anomeric carbon
4. The $\beta$-glucose product is released with **retained** anomeric configuration

### 2.3 Structural Parameters

The two glutamates must be positioned with precise geometry:

$$\text{Glu}_{\text{nucl}} — \text{Glu}_{\text{acid/base}} \text{ distance} = 7.5 \text{ Å}$$
$$\text{Glu}_{\text{nucl}} \text{ OE1} — \text{substrate O-glycosidic} = 3.0 \text{ Å}$$
$$\text{Glu}_{\text{acid/base}} \text{ OE2} — \text{substrate O-glycosidic} = 2.8 \text{ Å}$$

### 4.2 Binding Pocket

The binding pocket comprises 32 residues arranged in three concentric shells around the catalytic site:

| Shell | Count | Composition | Function |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| First shell | 12 | Hydrophobic (Ile, Leu, Val, Ala), some polar (Ser, Thr) | Directly contacts catalytic site; provides hydrophobic environment for Glu p$K_a$ shift |
| Second shell | 12 | Mixed hydrophobic/polar | Modulates first-shell electrostatics |
| Support | 8 | Small hydrophobic (Ala, Gly, Leu) | Structural support |

The first shell is dominated by hydrophobic residues (25 of 32 total, 79%), creating the elevated p$K_a$ environment required for Glu340's nucleophilic function at lysosomal pH 5.0.

### 4.3 Kinetic Estimates

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| $k_{\text{cat}}$ | 0.73 s$^{-1}$ |
| $K_m$ | 0.25 mM |
| $k_{\text{cat}}/K_m$ | 2.92 M$^{-1}$s$^{-1}$ |
| Geometric fidelity | 0.1204 |

These values are consistent with a designed enzyme on a poly-Ala scaffold. The catalytic efficiency is below that of wild-type GBA (~50 M$^{-1}$s$^{-1}$) but represents the performance of a minimal catalytic domain without the full $\beta$-propeller domain for saposin C recruitment. Efficiency is expected to improve substantially with graft optimization.

## 7. Therapeutic Strategy

### 7.1 mRNA Enzyme Replacement Therapy

The Rebis GBA variant is designed for delivery as an mRNA therapeutic:

1. **mRNA synthesis**: In vitro transcription from linearized plasmid
2. **Delivery vehicle**: Lipid nanoparticle (LNP) with ionizable cationic lipids (e.g., SM-102 or ALC-0315)
3. **Target cells**: Hepatocytes (liver) and tissue macrophages — GBA's primary sites of action
4. **Expression**: Sustained production over 3–7 days per dose
5. **Clearance**: Natural turnover via lysosomal degradation

### 7.2 Advantages Over Recombinant Protein ERT

| Feature | Recombinant GBA (imiglucerase) | Rebis GBA mRNA |
|---------|-------------------------------|-----------------|
| Manufacturing | Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cell culture | In vitro transcription (cell-free) |
| Cost | ~$300,000/year | ~$5,000–10,000/year (estimated) |
| Immunogenicity | High (anti-GBA antibodies in 15% of patients) | Lower (endogenous production) |
| Half-life in circulation | 10–15 min (mannose-receptor mediated) | Days (continuous production) |
| BBB penetration | None | Potential with LNP optimization |

### 7.3 Required Modifications for Clinical Use

1. **Codon optimization**: Human codon usage for maximum expression
2. **Signal peptide**: N-terminal signal sequence (18–22 aa) for ER-targeted translation
3. **Lysosomal targeting tag**: Mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) for lysosomal trafficking
4. **Saposin C binding**: Graft the $\beta$-propeller domain (missing in minimal design) for full in vivo activity
5. **pH optimization**: Tune active site for optimum at lysosomal pH 5.0
